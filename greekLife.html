    1. Alpha Delta Pi (ΑΔΠ)

    Alpha Delta Pi was founded on May 15, 1851, at Wesleyan Female College in Macon, Georgia, making it the first secret society for women. Originally named the Adelphean Society, it adopted the Greek letters ΑΔΠ in 1905. The sorority emphasizes its four key principles: scholarship, leadership, service to others, and sisterhood. Alpha Delta Pi has more than 160 active collegiate chapters and alumnae associations throughout the U.S. and Canada. Its official philanthropy is Ronald McDonald House Charities, and members regularly volunteer and raise funds for the cause. The organization promotes academic success, leadership development, and lifelong friendships rooted in mutual respect and shared values.

    2. Sigma Chi (ΣΧ)

    Sigma Chi was founded in 1855 at Miami University in Oxford, Ohio. Known for its emphasis on character and integrity, the fraternity was formed as a result of a disagreement over who should be elected poet of the Erodelphian Literary Society, demonstrating its commitment to individual principles. Sigma Chi encourages members to live by the Jordan Standard, which includes traits like courage, courtesy, and fidelity. It has over 240 active chapters and over 300,000 lifetime members. One of its core programs is the Sigma Chi Leadership Institute, which provides professional development training. The fraternity's primary philanthropy is the Huntsman Cancer Foundation, through which it has pledged millions to cancer research and patient care.

    3. Delta Gamma (ΔΓ)

    Delta Gamma was established in 1873 at the Lewis School for Girls in Oxford, Mississippi. The founders—Anna Boyd, Mary Comfort, and Eva Webb—wanted to promote friendship and mutual aid among women. Delta Gamma has since grown to over 150 collegiate chapters and more than 200 alumnae groups across North America. The sorority's official motto is "Do Good," and its members live by this principle through their philanthropic focus on Service for Sight, which supports the visually impaired and funds sight conservation research. Delta Gamma places strong emphasis on academic excellence, leadership training, and personal development, with structured programs to encourage civic responsibility and sisterhood.

    4. Phi Delta Theta (ΦΔΘ)

    Founded in 1848 at Miami University in Ohio, Phi Delta Theta is a men's fraternity with a foundation built on friendship, sound learning, and rectitude. Known as part of the "Miami Triad" (along with Beta Theta Pi and Sigma Chi), it helped shape the early Greek system in North America. The fraternity currently has more than 190 chapters and colonies and over 270,000 initiated members. Phi Delta Theta introduced the Alcohol-Free Housing initiative in 2000, a pioneering move in the Greek world. Its philanthropic efforts center around ALS awareness and support through the LiveLikeLou Foundation, named in honor of baseball legend and fraternity brother Lou Gehrig.

    5. Kappa Alpha Theta (ΚΑΘ)

    Kappa Alpha Theta was the first Greek-letter fraternity for women, established at DePauw University in 1870. Its founders wanted a group that promoted intellectual ambition and integrity. Today, Theta has more than 140 college chapters and 200 alumnae chapters. The organization focuses on personal excellence, leadership, service, and academic achievement. Thetas are active in advocacy for children through their national philanthropy, CASA (Court Appointed Special Advocates), which supports abused or neglected children navigating the court system. Members are encouraged to lead lives of purpose and to contribute meaningfully to their communities throughout and beyond college.

    6. Alpha Phi Alpha (ΑΦΑ)

    Alpha Phi Alpha was founded on December 4, 1906, at Cornell University in Ithaca, New York, and was the first intercollegiate fraternity for African American men. The seven founders, known as the “Seven Jewels,” created the organization as a support system for minority students facing racial prejudice. Alpha Phi Alpha has played a pivotal role in civil rights history, with notable members such as Dr. Martin Luther King Jr. and Thurgood Marshall. Its national programs include Go-To-High-School, Go-To-College, and A Voteless People is a Hopeless People. With over 700 chapters, Alpha Phi Alpha continues to emphasize leadership, scholarship, and service on both local and national scales.

    7. Zeta Tau Alpha (ΖΤΑ)

    Founded in 1898 at the State Female Normal School (now Longwood University) in Farmville, Virginia, Zeta Tau Alpha promotes friendship and service through its nine key values, including loyalty, humility, and responsibility. With over 260,000 initiated members and more than 170 active collegiate chapters, ZTA is one of the largest women's fraternities. Its national philanthropy is breast cancer education and awareness, in partnership with organizations like the American Cancer Society and the NFL. Members engage in Think Pink® events and campaigns to raise awareness and funds. ZTA fosters leadership and community involvement through structured training and sisterhood events.

    8. Kappa Sigma (ΚΣ)

    Kappa Sigma was founded in 1869 at the University of Virginia, but traces its roots to a secret society formed at the University of Bologna in the 1400s. With a strong tradition of academic achievement and personal growth, Kappa Sigma upholds four pillars: fellowship, leadership, scholarship, and service. It is one of the largest men’s fraternities with over 300,000 initiates and 300 chapters. Its philanthropic initiative, the Military Heroes Campaign, supports wounded veterans and their families. Kappa Sigma also runs programs for career development, financial literacy, and leadership. The fraternity encourages members to be scholars, gentlemen, and responsible citizens.

    9. Alpha Kappa Alpha (ΑΚΑ)

    Alpha Kappa Alpha, founded in 1908 at Howard University, is the first Greek-letter sorority established by African American women. With over 1,000 chapters and nearly 300,000 members worldwide, AKA has long been a force for educational, social, and economic empowerment. The sorority’s initiatives include HBCU support, women’s healthcare, financial literacy, and environmental stewardship. Alpha Kappa Alpha has consistently been at the forefront of civic engagement, and its members have made significant contributions in education, politics, business, and the arts. Its community service and mentorship programs aim to uplift underrepresented communities and promote academic excellence.
